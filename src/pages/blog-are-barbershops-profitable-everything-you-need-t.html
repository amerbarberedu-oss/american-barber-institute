Are Barbershops Profitable? The Real Numbers Behind the Chair

A decision guide for anyone weighing barbering as a business — not just a job.

Short answer: Yes — barbershops can be genuinely profitable, but profit is driven by a handful of controllable factors: your skill level and pricing, whether you rent a booth or work on commission, your location, and how well you retain clients. A skilled barber with a full book and smart cost control earns a solid living; a shop owner who manages rent, staffing and product sales well can do considerably better. The key is understanding the economics before you invest time and tuition.

What actually drives profit

Before chasing strategies to boost margin, understand the levers that determine your income — first as an individual barber, then as an owner.

  • Skill and pricing. Higher, more versatile skill lets you charge more per service. Training that covers many hair types and techniques directly widens your earning ceiling.
  • Client care and loyalty. Attentive service earns bigger tips, repeat visits and referrals — the cheapest and most reliable growth there is.
  • Booth rent vs. commission. How you're structured changes your take-home dramatically (see the comparison below).
  • Location. Rent and what a neighborhood will pay for a cut both flow from where you set up. The right location balances foot traffic against overhead.

Booth rent vs. commission: how the math differs

Most barbers earn under one of two models. Neither is "better" universally — it depends on your client volume and appetite for risk.

ModelHow it worksBest forTrade-off
CommissionYou keep a percentage of each service; the shop takes the rest.New barbers still building a client book.Lower ceiling; the shop keeps a cut of every dollar.
Booth rentYou pay a fixed monthly fee and keep 100% of what you earn.Established barbers with a steady, full book.You owe rent whether or not the chair is busy.

A common progression: start on commission to build clients with low risk, then move to booth rent once your book is full enough that keeping 100% beats splitting it.

The owner's view: shop-level profitability

Owning a shop stacks new costs on top of your own chair — lease, utilities, insurance, equipment, inventory and payroll. But it also unlocks income streams a single barber can't touch: a percentage of each employee's sales, rent from other barbers' booths, and retail product margin. Owners who manage those levers well turn a shop into a business, not just a bigger job. Expect it to take at least a year — often longer — to recoup the initial investment, so build that runway into your plan.

Frequently asked questions

Are barbershops a profitable business?

Yes, when run well. Profit depends on pricing, client retention, cost control (especially rent) and location — all of which the owner can influence.

Is it better to rent a booth or work on commission?

Commission suits barbers still building a book because it's low-risk; booth rent suits established barbers with a full schedule who want to keep 100% of their earnings.

How long until a new barbershop turns a profit?

Plan for at least a year to recoup start-up costs. Building a business plan and a realistic cash runway before you open is essential.
