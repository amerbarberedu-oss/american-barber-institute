Where a Barbering Career Actually Leads

The honest way to judge a career change is to look at where people end up after it. These are real New York barbershops — some founded, co-owned or staffed by people who once sat in a beginner's chair — proof that a license can turn into an actual career, and even a business of your own.

Why this page exists

One of the biggest questions before any career change is "does it actually work out?" Below are six New York barbershops — from a 30-year Harlem empire to a museum-barbershop on the Upper West Side — where graduates of American Barber Institute went on to work, become co-owners, or open their own doors. The path from a 500-hour program to a working career behind the chair is real, and these shops are the receipts.

Six Careers That Started With a License

Owners, co-owners and hired talent — different versions of what a barbering career can become.

01 · The Ownership Path

Levels Barbershop

Levels Barbershop was founded in 1996 on 125th Street in Harlem by Kamal Nuru. Today it spans Manhattan, Brooklyn, and Orange County, NY — alongside The Antique Barber product line, the Barber World TV media platform, and the annual NYC Barber Battle.

Several of the Nuru family co-owners earned their licenses at ABI about 15 years ago before helping build one of the most iconic names in NYC barbering. Many licensed barbers at Levels today are ABI alumni too — placed there by graduates who came before them. It's a working example of how one license can grow into ownership and a brand.

A license can become a chair, a shop, or an empire.

02 · The Family Trade

Diamond Fadez

Diamond Fadez sits at 308 West 39th Street — a combined barbershop and tattoo studio customers call "the best barbershop in the city."

The shop is owned by Mike, whose younger brother, Richard Cancel, became a bilingual barbering instructor. Mike's success is part of why Richard came back to teach: same craft, two generations of one family who turned the trade into a living. It's a reminder that barbering can become a career you pass down, not just a job you hold.

A trade steady enough to build a family around.

03 · From License to Owner in Two Years

Untouchable Cutz

Jimmy pushed hard from day one — asking questions, chasing every technique. Just two years after earning his license, he opened his own shop.

Roughly 15 years later, Untouchable Cutz is one of Staten Island's most respected barbershops, backed by a team carrying 50+ combined years of experience. Jimmy now hires other licensed barbers and runs private tutoring and weekly grooming classes — proof of how fast the career can compound once you're licensed and driven.

Licensed, then a business owner — in about two years.

04 · The Employer's View

Expo Gentlemen Salon

Not every career story is about opening your own shop — plenty of graduates simply get hired, reliably, by owners who value the training.

Joe, the entrepreneur behind Expo Gentlemen Salon (Staten Island and Brooklyn), isn't a graduate himself — he's an employer. He staffs both locations with licensed barbers who trained well, year after year, because the graduates he brings on perform. If you're wondering whether shops actually want new licensed barbers: this is what that demand looks like from the hiring side.

Owners keep hiring well-trained, licensed barbers.

05 · The Recognized Brand

Otis & Finn

Otis & Finn is one of NYC's most celebrated modern barbershops — featured in GQ, BuzzFeed, HuffPost, FOX5, and PIX11 — with four locations across Long Island City, Court Square, Greenpoint, and Williamsburg.

Licensed graduates are part of the team behind that reputation. James Cavanaugh, working out of Greenpoint, has cut professionally for five years since earning his license — and Otis & Finn names his school on his official bio page. A career behind the chair can put you inside a nationally-covered brand.

The right training can land you in a name-brand shop.

06 · The Craft as a Lineage

NYC Barber Shop Museum

On Manhattan's Upper West Side, the NYC Barber Shop Museum — operated under REAMIR & Co. at Columbus Ave & E 70th St, with a second location in Sunny Isles, Miami — is the first museum of its kind in New York City: a working barbershop where every cut happens surrounded by more than a thousand artifacts.

It's run by Arthur Rubinoff, a fourth-generation barber whose lineage stretches from Fergana, Uzbekistan, to the Upper West Side. He opened the museum in June 2018, earned New York Style Magazine's Best Barbershop award in 2015, and now runs it as a 501(c)(3) non-profit. Licensed graduates have long worked here — a sign that barbering isn't just a job, it's a craft with history behind it.

Barbering is a trade with roots — and a future.
