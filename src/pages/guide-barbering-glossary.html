The Barbering Glossary: 45+ Barber Terms & Techniques Defined

Every term a new barber, student or client needs — fades, tapers, texturizing, lineups, guards and more — defined in plain English by working instructors.

Quick answer

Barbering has its own vocabulary — a fade blends hair from short to long with no visible line, a taper gradually shortens hair at the edges, texturizing removes bulk without changing length, and a lineup creates crisp edges along the hairline. This glossary defines 45+ of the most common barbering terms, techniques and tools, grouped by category so you can find what you need fast.

By ABI Instructional Staff · Reviewed by a licensed New York Master Barber · Last updated 2026

Whether you're a student learning the trade, a career-changer researching it, or a client who wants to speak your barber's language, these are the words you'll hear on the floor. Jump to a section:

Category 01

Cuts & Fades

Fade

A haircut where the hair blends seamlessly from very short at the bottom to longer at the top, with no hard line between lengths. The signature modern barber skill.

Low Fade

A fade that begins low on the head, just above the ears and neckline, for a subtle, conservative blend.

Mid Fade

A fade that starts around the temples — the most versatile, popular height, balanced between low and high.

High Fade

A fade that begins high on the sides, creating strong contrast between the short sides and longer top.

Skin / Bald Fade

A fade taken all the way down to bare skin at the bottom, usually finished with a foil shaver for a clean, smooth blend.

Drop Fade

A fade that curves or "drops" lower behind the ear, following the head's shape for a rounded look.

Burst Fade

A fade that fans out in a semicircle around the ear, leaving length at the back — common with mohawks and mullets.

Taper

A gradual shortening of the hair at the edges (neckline and sideburns). Unlike a fade, a taper keeps more length and is more subtle.

Taper Fade

A hybrid combining a taper's gentle edge shortening with a fade's blend — clean but not extreme.

Crew Cut

A short, tapered cut, slightly longer at the front and graduating shorter toward the back.

Buzz Cut

A very short, uniform cut done with clippers and a single guard length all over.

Pompadour

A style with significant volume swept up and back from the forehead, usually with shorter sides.

Undercut

Short or shaved sides with a distinct, disconnected longer length on top — no blending between the two.

Quiff

A style with the front hair lifted up and back for volume, less structured than a pompadour.

Category 02

Techniques

Lineup (Edge-up / Shape-up)

Creating sharp, defined edges along the hairline, temples and beard with a trimmer or razor. The detail that makes a cut look crisp.

Blending

Smoothing the transition between different lengths so there's no visible line — the core skill behind every good fade.

Texturizing

Removing bulk and weight from the hair to add movement and softness without changing the overall length — often done with texturizing shears or point cutting.

Point Cutting

Cutting into the ends of the hair at an angle with the shear tips to soften lines and create texture.

Scissor-Over-Comb

Lifting hair with a comb and cutting along it with shears — a classic technique for blending and controlling short-to-medium lengths.

Clipper-Over-Comb

The same idea as scissor-over-comb but using clippers instead of shears, for faster blending on shorter hair.

Guard (Comb Attachment)

A plastic or metal attachment that clips onto clippers to leave a consistent length — numbered (e.g. #1, #2) by length in eighths of an inch.

Guest Consultation

The conversation before the cut where the barber learns what the client wants, checks hair type and growth patterns, and sets expectations.

Neckline

The bottom edge of the hair at the back of the neck, shaped square, rounded or tapered (natural) to finish the cut.

Cowlick

A section of hair that grows in a different direction from the rest, which the barber must work with rather than against.

Sanitation & Disinfection

Cleaning and disinfecting tools and stations between clients — a core safety practice tested on the State Board exam and required by law.

Hot Towel Shave

A traditional straight-razor shave using hot towels to soften the beard and open pores — a premium barber service.

Category 03

Tools & Terms

Clippers

The primary cutting tool for removing bulk hair and building the base of a cut. Corded or cordless.

Trimmer (Outliner / T-liner)

A smaller tool with a fine blade for detail work — lineups, edges and beard shaping.

Foil Shaver

An electric shaver used to take hair down to smooth skin, especially for bald fades and clean finishes.

Shears

Professional barbering scissors. Cutting shears handle length and scissor-over-comb work; blending shears have teeth to remove weight.

Straight Razor / Shavette

A blade used for the crispest lineups and traditional shaves. A shavette uses disposable blades for hygiene.

Lever (Taper Lever)

The switch on a clipper that fine-tunes cutting length between guard sizes without changing the guard.

Blade Wash

A cleaning solution that clears hair and buildup from clipper blades and helps cool them.

Barbicide

The blue disinfectant solution barbers keep tools in — the industry standard for sanitizing combs and implements.

Neck Duster

A soft brush used to sweep away loose hair from the client's neck and face.

Cape

The protective covering draped over the client to keep hair off their clothes.

Neck Strip

A disposable paper strip placed around the neck under the cape for hygiene and comfort.

Category 04

Business & Career Terms

Master Barber License

In New York, the license earned after 500 training hours and passing the State Board exam. It qualifies you to work anywhere in the state and to own a shop and employ barbers.

State Board Exam

The written and practical examination, administered under the NYS Division of Licensing Services, you must pass to be licensed.

Booth Rent

An arrangement where a barber pays the shop a fixed weekly rent for their chair and keeps everything they earn — rewards a full, fast book.

Commission

A pay model where the barber splits each ticket with the shop (often 50–60% to the barber) — the common low-risk starting point.

Book (Clientele)

A barber's base of returning clients. A full book of loyal regulars is the single biggest driver of stable income.

Rebooking

Scheduling a client's next appointment before they leave — the habit that turns one-off cuts into a dependable book.

Walk-in

A client who arrives without an appointment. Walk-ins are how new barbers first build their clientele.

Ticket

The total charge for a client's service(s) — the amount split under commission or kept under booth rent.

Apprentice

Someone learning barbering under a licensed barber. New York recognizes both school-based training and apprenticeship paths to licensure.

ACCES-VR

New York State's vocational rehabilitation program, which can fund barber training for qualifying students with a documented disability.

Common Questions

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between a fade and a taper?

A taper gradually shortens hair at the edges while keeping noticeable length — subtle and conservative. A fade blends hair from very short (even down to skin) up to longer on top with no visible line. Every fade is a kind of taper, but not every taper is a fade.

What does texturizing mean in barbering?

Texturizing removes bulk and weight from the hair to add movement and softness without changing the overall length. Barbers do it with texturizing (blending) shears or by point cutting into the ends.

What is a lineup in a haircut?

A lineup (also called an edge-up or shape-up) is the crisp, defined edge a barber creates along the hairline, temples and beard using a trimmer or razor. It's the finishing detail that makes a cut look sharp.

What do the numbers on clipper guards mean?

Guard numbers indicate how much hair they leave, measured in eighths of an inch — a #1 leaves 1/8", a #2 leaves 1/4", and so on. Higher numbers leave longer hair. They give consistent, repeatable lengths.
