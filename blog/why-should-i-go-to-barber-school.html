Is Barber School Worth It? An Honest Guide for 2026

By David Ayeoribe · Lead Senior Instructor & Director · February 1, 2024

A decision guide for career-changers weighing the leap into barbering.

Short answer: For most people who want a hands-on trade with a fast path to earning, self-employment potential and recession-resistant demand, barber school is worth it. In New York you'll invest 500 hours of state-approved training — roughly 4 months full-time or 6–7 months on weekends — before you can sit for licensure. Whether it pays off for you depends on five things: your timeline, your budget, your appetite for self-employment, your people skills, and your local market.

Who barbering is actually a good fit for

Barbering rewards a specific kind of person. Before you spend a dollar on tuition, it helps to be honest about whether the day-to-day suits you.

  • You like working with your hands and seeing a finished result. Every client is a project you complete in 30–45 minutes.
  • You're comfortable talking to strangers all day. The chair is as much conversation as clippers — repeat clients come back for you, not just the cut.
  • You want control over your schedule and income. Barbering has one of the clearest routes from employee to booth-renter to shop owner of any skilled trade.
  • You'd rather earn while you learn a craft than sit through years of general-education classes. A focused 500-hour program gets you licensed far faster than most degree paths.

The case for going: five reasons it holds up

1. The demand doesn't disappear

People need haircuts in every economy. Barbering is one of the more recession-resistant service trades precisely because the work can't be offshored, automated, or indefinitely postponed. A skill that people pay for every three to four weeks is a durable one.

2. A short, direct path to earning

Unlike careers that require two- or four-year degrees, licensure in New York is built on 500 hours of training — completable in about 4 months full-time. You start building a client book and income far sooner than most fields allow.

3. A real route to owning your income

Barbering has a well-worn ladder: work a chair, rent a booth, then open your own shop. Few trades let you move from employee to business owner as clearly. If being your own boss is the goal, this is one of the more attainable on-ramps.

4. It's a craft you keep improving at

Cuts, fades, beard work, textures and hair types are a skill set you refine for years. That constant improvement keeps the work engaging — and the more versatile your skills, the fuller your chair stays.

5. Community comes built in

Barbershops are social hubs, and the profession has a strong culture of mentorship and referral. The people you train alongside often become the network that lands your first job and your first booth.

The honest trade-offs

A fair decision weighs the costs too. Barbering is on-your-feet work; income in the early months depends on how fast you build repeat clients; and self-employment means handling your own taxes, supplies and slow seasons. None of these are dealbreakers — but going in aware of them is the difference between a career and a false start.

How to make the decision

  1. Check the money. Read how much barbers actually earn so your expectations match reality.
  2. Price the training. See what barber school costs in NYC and which funding options apply to you.
  3. Look past graduation. Understand what happens after barber school and the earning ladder that follows.
  4. Confirm the requirements. Verify New York's licensing hours and exam through the state before committing.

Frequently asked questions

How long does barber school take in New York?

New York requires 500 hours of state-approved training — about 4 months full-time or 6–7 months on a weekend schedule.

Is barbering a stable career?

Yes. Demand is steady and can't be automated or outsourced, which makes barbering more recession-resistant than many service jobs.

Do I need a college degree to become a barber?

No. You need to complete a state-approved training program and pass New York's licensing exam — no college degree is required.

Can barbering lead to owning a business?

Yes. Many barbers progress from working a chair to renting a booth to owning a shop, one of the clearest small-business paths in the trades.
