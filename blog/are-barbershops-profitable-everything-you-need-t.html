Are Barbershops Profitable? The Real Numbers Behind the Chair

By David Ayeoribe · Lead Senior Instructor & Director · February 1, 2024

Yes — barbershops can be genuinely profitable, but profit isn't luck. It's driven by a handful of controllable factors: your skill level and pricing, whether you rent a booth or work on commission, your location, and how well you retain clients. A skilled barber with a full book and smart cost control earns a solid living; a shop owner who manages rent, staffing and product sales well can do considerably better. The key is understanding the economics before you invest time and tuition — not after.

This is a decision guide for anyone weighing barbering as a business, not just a job. We'll walk the levers that move the money, the two pay structures every barber chooses between, and the added costs and income streams that come with owning a shop — so you can price the opportunity honestly.

The levers that move the money

What actually drives profit

Before chasing tactics to boost margin, understand the four levers that determine your income — first as an individual barber, then as an owner. Get these right and the rest is refinement.

Skill & pricing

Range widens the ceiling

Higher, more versatile skill lets you charge more per service and serve more of the room. Training across many hair types, textures and techniques — fades, tapers, beard work, scissor-over-comb, coily and straight hair alike — directly widens your earning ceiling. The barber who can cut anyone who walks in never turns away revenue.

Loyalty

Retention is the cheapest growth

Attentive service earns bigger tips, repeat visits and word-of-mouth referrals — the most reliable and lowest-cost growth there is. Winning a new client costs far more than keeping an existing one, so a rebooked chair is pure margin. Consistency, not gimmicks, fills a book.

Chair setup

Booth rent vs. commission

How your chair is structured changes your take-home dramatically. Commission trades a share of every dollar for lower risk; booth rent trades a fixed monthly cost for keeping everything you earn. See the full side-by-side comparison below before you commit.

Location

Rent vs. what the block pays

Rent and what a neighborhood will pay for a cut both flow from where you set up. The right spot balances foot traffic and ticket price against overhead — a busy corner that supports higher prices can out-earn a cheap lease with no walk-ins. In a market like New York, block-by-block economics matter.

The pay-structure decision

Booth rent vs. commission: how the math differs

Most barbers earn under one of two models. Neither is "better" universally — the right choice depends on your client volume and your appetite for risk.

Model How it works Best for Trade-off
CommissionYou keep a percentage of each service; the shop takes the rest.New barbers still building a client book.Lower ceiling; the shop keeps a cut of every dollar.
Booth rentYou pay a fixed monthly fee and keep 100% of what you earn.Established barbers with a steady, full book.You owe rent whether or not the chair is busy.

A common progression: start on commission to build clients with low risk, then move to booth rent once your book is full enough that keeping 100% beats splitting it. The switch point is simple math — when your commission cut on a full week exceeds a month's booth rent, renting starts to pay.

"Commission buys you a safety net while you build a book. Booth rent rewards you once the book is built."

From chair to business

The owner's view: shop-level profitability

Owning a shop stacks new costs on top of your own chair — lease, utilities, insurance, equipment, inventory and payroll. But it also unlocks income streams a single barber can't touch: a percentage of each employee's sales, rent from other barbers' booths, and retail product margin. Owners who manage those levers well turn a shop into a business, not just a bigger job.

Expect it to take at least a year — often longer — to recoup the initial investment, so build that runway into your plan. The shops that survive their first year are the ones that opened with a realistic cash cushion and a clear sense of their fixed monthly burn.

"A single barber sells time. An owner sells time, space, and product — three margins instead of one."

The bottom line

Is it worth the investment for you?

The return on barber training is real, but it's not automatic — it rewards skill, consistency and business sense. If you're weighing the decision, work through the money side first, in order:

  1. See how much barbers earn at each stage of a career.
  2. Price the entry cost in what barber school costs in NYC.
  3. Understand the earning ladder in what happens after barber school.

Barber training itself is a defined, front-loaded investment. New York's licensing path runs on a 500-hour curriculum — roughly 4 months full-time or about 6–7 months on a weekend schedule — leading to a NYS-licensed credential. Compared with the multi-year cost of most careers, that's a short runway to a skill that earns from day one behind the chair.

Answers

Frequently asked questions

Are barbershops a profitable business?

Yes, when run well. Profit depends on pricing, client retention, cost control (especially rent) and location — all of which the owner can influence. Shops that struggle usually do so on the cost side or by failing to keep chairs full, not because the demand isn't there.

Is it better to rent a booth or work on commission?

Commission suits barbers still building a book because it's low-risk — you only pay when you earn. Booth rent suits established barbers with a full schedule who want to keep 100% of their earnings. Many barbers start on commission and switch to booth rent once their book is consistently full.

How long until a new barbershop turns a profit?

Plan for at least a year to recoup start-up costs, and often longer. Building a business plan and a realistic cash runway before you open is essential — the shops that make it are the ones that budgeted for a slow ramp instead of assuming an instant full house.
