Training at the Bronx Campus: What It's Really Like at Westchester Square

A first-hand look at learning to barber in the east Bronx — the clinic floor, the neighborhood, who trains here, and exactly how to get to 121 Westchester Square.

The short answer: ABI's Bronx campus sits at 121 Westchester Square in the east Bronx, a short walk from the 6 train. Training there is overwhelmingly hands-on: after the fundamentals, you spend most of your 500 hours cutting real, paying clients on a supervised clinic floor while an instructor guides you. Over about 4 months full-time (or 6–7 months on weekends) you go from first fade to State-Board ready — close to home, in a neighborhood that lives and breathes barbering.

By Denise Okafor, Barbering Program Advisor · Reviewed by the ABI instruction team · Last updated 2026

Why train in the Bronx

The Bronx is a barbering borough. Shops line the avenues, styles move fast, and the clientele is as varied as any place in the country — which is exactly what makes it a serious place to learn. Training here means you build your skills on the kind of real, diverse heads of hair you'll see the day you start working. For a lot of students it's also simply the practical choice: it's close to home, close to the 6 train, and it means less time commuting and more time on the clippers.

What a day on the Bronx clinic floor looks like

The biggest surprise for new students is how little of the day is spent sitting and listening. Barbering is a skill of the hands, so the training is built around doing. You get the theory you need — sanitation, skin and hair science, cutting principles, New York law — but the heart of the program is the clinic floor, where you cut real clients under an instructor's eye. Here's the honest shape of a training day.

Getting to 121 Westchester Square

The campus is in Westchester Square, a walkable, well-connected pocket of the east Bronx. However you commute, it's an easy trip.

Who you'll train alongside

No single kind of person trains here — and that's the point. On any given day the Bronx floor includes:

  • Career-changers leaving a job that never fit, betting on a trade they can own.
  • Recent grads who'd rather learn a skill than sit in a lecture hall.
  • Parents and workers fitting training around a full life on the weekend or afternoon schedule.
  • Veterans using GI Bill® benefits to fund a second career.
  • ACCES-VR participants whose training can be state-funded.

Many come from the east Bronx and Westchester because it's close to home. What they share is simpler than any label: the drive to work and the willingness to show up. If you're still weighing it, our "is barber training right for you?" check is a good place to start.

Why the clinic floor changes everything

Cutting a mannequin and cutting a nervous first-time client are different skills. On the Bronx clinic floor you practice the whole job — the consultation, the conversation, the timing, the pressure of getting it right on a real head of hair — while it's still safe to make and fix mistakes. By graduation you're not someone who can cut hair; you're someone who already has, on diverse hair types, hundreds of times. That's the difference between a certificate and a career.

Fitting training around your Bronx life

The 500-hour requirement is fixed; the pace is not. Full-time students finish in about four months. Weekend students finish in roughly six to seven months while keeping a job. Every track leads to the same license — the only thing that changes is how it fits your week. Frequently asked

Where exactly is the Bronx campus?

121 Westchester Square, Bronx, NY 10461 — in the Westchester Square neighborhood of the east Bronx, a short walk from the 6 train and served by several bus lines.

What's the best way to get there?

The 6 train to Westchester Square–East Tremont Avenue is closest. The Bx4, Bx8, Bx31, Bx40 and Bx42 buses serve the area, and there's street parking for drivers coming off the Cross Bronx or Bruckner Expressways.

Is the training the same as the Manhattan campus?

Yes — same 500-hour program, same hands-on clinic-floor approach, and the same New York Master Barber license. The difference is location and the local clientele you learn on.

How long does it take and how many hours?

New York requires 500 training hours. That's about 4 months full-time or 6–7 months on a weekend schedule, followed by the State Board exam.

Can I visit before I decide?

Absolutely. You can book a tour to walk the clinic floor, meet instructors, and ask questions. New classes begin the first Monday of every month, so it helps to visit early.

New York barbering licenses and exams are administered by the New York Department of State, Division of Licensing Services.
