How to Become a Barber in New York: The Steps, Hours & License

By David Ayeoribe · Lead Senior Instructor & Director · February 1, 2024

To become a licensed barber in New York you complete a state-approved 500-hour Master Barber course, finish the required 3-hour HIV/AIDS & Contagious Diseases component, and pass the New York State Board exam. Full-time that takes about 4 months; on a weekend schedule about 6–7 months. Once you pass and are licensed you can start working — and, with a Master Barber license, eventually own your own shop.

Thinking about a career change into barbering? The path is clearer than most people expect. Here is every step, in order, so you know exactly what becoming a barber in New York involves — the hours, the license, the cost, and the timeline — before you commit a single dollar or day.

The path at a glance

The two numbers that define the journey

Barbering in New York runs on a fixed, state-set standard. Before the steps, here are the headline figures that shape every schedule, cost, and timeline decision you will make.

0 hours of state-approved training required for the NY Master Barber license
0 months to finish full-time (about 6–7 months on a weekend schedule)
0 minimum age to enroll, with an 8th-grade equivalent education

No prior experience is required. If you can commit to the 500 hours and pass the State Board exam, the license — and everything it unlocks — is within reach.

Step by step

The steps to becoming a barber in New York

Five steps take you from "I'm thinking about it" to licensed and earning behind a chair. None of them are complicated — they simply have to be done in order.

Step 1

Meet the basics

You need to be at least 17 years old and have the equivalent of an 8th-grade education. No prior experience is required — most students walk in having never held professional clippers, and that is exactly what the program is for.

Step 2

Enroll in a 500-hour Master Barber program

New York requires 500 hours of state-approved training. This is where you learn cutting, fades and tapers, shaves, lineups, sanitation, and the theory the State Board exam tests — the full technical foundation of the trade.

Step 3

Complete the 3-hour Contagious Diseases course

New York also mandates a short 3-hour HIV/AIDS & Contagious Diseases component. It ensures every licensed barber can maintain a safe, hygienic environment for clients — a small requirement with real weight at the chair.

Step 4

Pass the New York State Board exam

A written and practical test covering technique, safety, and sanitation. A program that weaves exam prep through the whole curriculum — rather than cramming it at the end — is the one that gets students through on the first try.

Step 5

Get licensed and start working

Once licensed you can work behind a chair immediately — and a Master Barber license also qualifies you to own a shop and employ other barbers, which is where the real ceiling on income lives.

Timeline

How long does it take?

The 500 hours are fixed — but how fast you clear them is up to you. Programs offer morning, afternoon, and weekend schedules, so most people fit training around the life they already have.

Because the schedule flexes to your life instead of the other way around, barbering is one of the most accessible career changes available in New York — you can train toward a new trade without walking away from the income you have today.

"The 500 hours are set by the state — but the schedule is yours. Full-time in about four months, or weekends over six or seven, without quitting your day job."

Inside the training

What you'll actually learn

A good 500-hour program is far more than watching demos. You train on a real, diverse clientele and build a complete, sellable toolkit.

The technical range covers classic and modern cutting, fades and tapers, shear and razor work, lineups, straight-razor and hot-towel shaves, styling across every hair type and texture, color basics, sanitation, and the theory the New York State Board tests. Just as important, the strongest programs teach the business side — clientele-building, professionalism, and how to keep a book full — so you graduate ready to earn, not just to pass an exam.

Range is what separates a half-full chair from a waitlist: the barber who can handle any hair that sits down never has to send a paying client across the street. Read more on the range of techniques barber training should cover, and see who guides you through it in our look at barber school instructors in NYC.

The investment

What does it cost — and can you afford it?

Barbering is one of the most accessible skilled trades to enter, and the way most students pay for it is a big part of why.

A licensed 500-hour New York program typically runs from around $4,500 to over $10,000, depending on the school. But most students never write a single large check — they pay with a weekly plan instead of a lump sum, often out of income they are still earning while training on a flexible schedule. Veterans and eligible New Yorkers can also draw on funding programs to offset the cost further.

That combination — a modest total, weekly payments, and available funding — is why so many career-changers can start training now rather than "someday." See the full breakdown in how much barber school costs in New York, and explore support options on our tuition and funding page.

The payoff

Is barbering a good career?

For the right person, yes. Barbering rewards skill, consistency, and hustle more directly than almost any trade with such a short runway to entry.

You can start earning right after licensing, set your own schedule, and grow toward booth rent or shop ownership — a path with a genuinely high income ceiling. There is no guaranteed salary, but there is also no cap: what you earn tracks the size and loyalty of the book you build. For barbers who treat it like a business, that is the appeal.

Questions, answered

Frequently asked questions

How do I become a barber in New York?

You complete a state-approved 500-hour Master Barber course, finish the required 3-hour HIV/AIDS & Contagious Diseases component, and pass the New York State Board exam. You must be at least 17 with the equivalent of an 8th-grade education; no prior experience is needed. Once you pass and are licensed, you can work behind a chair — and a Master Barber license also lets you own a shop.

How many hours of barber school do you need in New York?

New York requires 500 hours of state-approved training for the Master Barber license. On top of the 500 hours, you also complete a separate 3-hour HIV/AIDS & Contagious Diseases course. Full-time, the 500 hours take about 4 months; on a weekend schedule, about 6–7 months.

Do you need a license to be a barber in New York?

Yes. To cut hair professionally for pay in New York you must hold a state-issued barber license. Getting it means finishing the 500-hour Master Barber course and the required Contagious Diseases component, then passing the New York State Board exam. See the full checklist in our NY barber license requirements guide.

How long does it take to become a barber?

It depends on your schedule. The 500 required hours take about 4 months full-time or roughly 6–7 months on weekends, followed by the State Board exam. Because programs offer morning, afternoon, and weekend options, you can move as fast as your availability allows without changing the total number of hours.
