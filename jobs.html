Careers & Placement

Where a Barber License Actually Takes You

Thinking about barbering as a career? A New York barber license opens more doors than most people realize. This page breaks down what licensed barbers really do for a living — from a commission chair in a busy shop to owning the shop yourself — the job outlook, the money, and exactly how graduates line up work after they finish their 500 hours. Shop owners hiring trained talent will find a form below to plug straight into our Manhattan and Bronx graduate pipeline.

Hiring a Barber? See Career Paths

The Short Answer

Is Barbering a Career Worth Building?

Yes — for the right person. Licensed barbering is a skilled, in-demand, largely recession-resistant trade you can enter in about four months of full-time training (or six to seven months on weekends). It offers rare flexibility: work for a shop, rent a chair, go mobile, or own a business — and in most of those paths you control your own income and hours. It isn't passive money; your pay tracks your skill, your speed and your book of clients. But for people who want a hands-on craft with real upward mobility and no four-year-degree requirement, few careers compete.

Seven Ways to Earn

Career Paths Behind the Chair

One license, many directions. These are the most common ways licensed barbers build a living in New York — and the reason so many career-changers pick this trade.

Shop Barber (Commission)

Cut in an established shop and split earnings with the owner. The easiest way to start — steady foot traffic, no overhead, and you build your clientele on someone else's rent while you find your speed.

Chair / Booth Renter

Pay a flat weekly rent for a station and keep everything you earn. Higher ceiling, more responsibility — the classic step up once you have a following.

Shop Owner

Open your own barbershop and earn from your chair plus every chair you rent out. The biggest upside, and where many driven barbers ultimately head. See real examples on where graduates go.

Mobile / Home-Service Barber

Bring the cut to clients — busy professionals, events, elder care. Low overhead and premium pricing for convenience and a flexible schedule.

Specialist & Content Barber

Build a name around a signature skill — designs, color, straight-razor work — and grow a following that fills your chair and your feed.

Barber Instructor

With experience and a state instructor license, teach the next generation. A stable, respected path that keeps you in the craft — meet our instructors.

Educator / Brand Rep

Work with clipper and product brands as an ambassador, platform artist or trainer — travel, stage work and industry influence built on top of your license.

Why People Choose It

What Makes Barbering Different

People get haircuts in every economy — demand for skilled, licensed barbers doesn't clock out.

Fast to Enter

Job-ready in about four months of full-time training — no multi-year degree or student debt required.

Recession-Resistant

People get haircuts in every economy. Demand for skilled, licensed barbers stays steady through downturns.

You Control Your Income

Rent a chair or own a shop and your ceiling is your own — you decide how hard you work and how much you earn.

Flexible & Portable

A license travels. Set your own hours, go mobile, or relocate across New York and keep working.

The honest part: nobody hands you a full chair on day one. Income tracks skill, speed and reputation, and you build a clientele over time. Employers also weigh conduct and professionalism, not just cutting ability — which is exactly what good training prepares you for.

Careers, Answered

Common Questions About Barbering Jobs

Do barbershops actually hire new graduates?

Yes. Established shops routinely bring on newly licensed barbers on commission — it's how they fill chairs and grow their bench. What matters most to owners is that you're licensed, professional, and can handle the full range of services. See the shops where our graduates work on where graduates go.

How soon can I start earning after I get licensed?

Once you pass the New York State Board exam and hold your license, you can begin working immediately — typically on commission at an established shop while you build your clientele. Your income grows as your book grows.

What's the difference between commission and booth rent?

On commission you split each cut with the shop owner but carry no overhead — ideal when you're starting out. With booth rent you pay a flat weekly fee for your station and keep everything you earn — a higher ceiling once you have a steady following.
